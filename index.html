A third map of the Earth

Most globes show the outside: borders, coastlines, terrain. TERRASCAN looks inward. As you drive the depth probe from the surface to the inner core, two things are layered on top of each other:

  • The scan. A crisp, classified slice of what seismic tomography actually sees at that depth — fast (cold, sinking) and slow (hot, rising) shear-velocity anomalies. It is not binary “scanned / not scanned”: colour encodes the anomaly and transparency encodes how well the region is resolved. Where coverage is thin, the scan fades and the model behind shows through.
  • The model. A soft, blurry shell behind the scan — the smooth, theoretical reference Earth (the radially-symmetric PREM structure). It is deliberately out of focus: an estimation, not an observation.

An honest note on the data

The radial profile (velocities, density, pressure, layer boundaries) is the real PREM reference model (Dziewonski & Anderson, 1981). The lateral anomalies are a hand-built, geographically-faithful synthesis of well-established features from published global models (e.g. S40RTS, SEMUCB-WM1, GyPSuM) — the African & Pacific LLSVPs, subducted slabs, plume conduits, cratonic roots — rather than a pixel-exact re-render of any single dataset. Treat it as an illustrative map of what we know is down there, not a measurement.
